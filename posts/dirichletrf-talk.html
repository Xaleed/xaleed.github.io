Compositional Data

A simplex is a convex space of all vectors with non-negative components that sum to a constant.

\[S^c_k = \left\{ (y_1, \dots, y_k)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^k \;\middle|\; y_i \geq 0,\; \sum_{i=1}^k y_i = c \right\}\]

It is common to set c = 1. We refer to data from this space as compositional data.

Example: Consider Y = (50, 20, 30) — the number of students in mathematics, statistics, and computer science. The corresponding compositional vector is: \[\left(\frac{50}{100},\;\frac{20}{100},\;\frac{30}{100}\right) = (0.5,\; 0.2,\; 0.3)\]

Machine Learning: Random Forest

  • ▶ Why machine learning?
    For compositional responses: many predictors with complex non-linear patterns that traditional models cannot capture.
  • ▶ Common approaches:
    Apply ML without considering the simplex geometry, or transform data (log-ratio transformations) to real space — risking distortion and bias.
  • ▶ Why Random Forests?
    RFs are flexible and their splitting rules can be easily adapted to respect the simplex structure.

MSE Splitting & Its Limitation

Parent Node Covariate: \(X^{n\times m}\)
Response: \(Y^{n\times k}\)
(c, i) = ?
\(x_{ij}\leq c\)\(x_{ij}>c\)
Left Child \(Y^{n_1\times k}\)
MSE = \(\text{MSE}(Y^{n_1},c,i)\)
Right Child \(Y^{n_2\times k}\)
MSE = \(\text{MSE}(Y^{n_2},c,i)\)
\[(c^*, i^*) = \operatorname{argmin}_{i,c}\!\left(\text{MSE}(Y^{n_1},c,i)+\text{MSE}(Y^{n_2},c,i)\right)\]
⚠ Problem: In simplex space the component variance depends on the mean. Under the Dirichlet distribution: \[V(X_i)=\frac{E(X_i)\left(1-E(X_i)\right)}{\alpha_0+1}\] This violates the homoscedasticity assumption of MSE. → Solution: use Dirichlet log-likelihood.

Dirichlet Distribution

\[f_{\mathbf{Z}}((z_1,\ldots,z_k);\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha_0)}{\prod_{i=1}^k\Gamma(\alpha_i)}\prod_{i=1}^k z_i^{\alpha_i-1}, \quad z_i\in[0,1],\;\textstyle\sum z_i=1\]
  • ▶ The sum-constant requirement of the simplex is directly satisfied — no transformation needed.
  • ▶ Extensions available: Zero-inflated Dirichlet and Kummer–Dirichlet for special cases.
  • ▶ The concentration parameter \(\alpha_0 = \sum_{i=1}^k \alpha_i\) controls the spread of the distribution across the simplex.

Dirichlet Log-Likelihood Splitting

Parent Node \(X^{n\times m}\), \(Y^{n\times k}\)
(c, i) = ?
\(X_i\leq c\)\(X_i>c\)
Left Child \(Y^{n_1\times k}\)
\(\ell = \mathcal{L}(Y^{n_1},c,i)\)
Right Child \(Y^{n_2\times k}\)
\(\ell = \mathcal{L}(Y^{n_2},c,i)\)
\[(c^*, i^*) = \operatorname{argmax}_{i,c}\!\left(\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\mathcal{L}(Y^{n_1},c,i)+\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\mathcal{L}(Y^{n_2},c,i)\right)\]

With this modification we obtain the Dirichlet Random Forest (DirichletRF) — applicable directly to compositional data without any transformation.

Performance Evaluation

Transformation-based RF baselines
CLR-RF and ILR-RF: data transformed to log-ratio space → standard RF → predictions mapped back to simplex. \[\text{CLR}(\mathbf{y}) = \left(\ln\frac{y_1}{g(\mathbf{y})},\ldots,\ln\frac{y_k}{g(\mathbf{y})}\right);\quad g(\mathbf{y})=\left(\prod_{i=1}^k y_i\right)^{1/k}\] \[\text{ILR}(\mathbf{y}) = \text{CLR}(\mathbf{y})\,\Phi,\quad \Phi\text{ orthonormal basis}\]
1. Aitchison Distance
\[AC = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^k\left(\log\frac{Y_{ij}}{g(\mathbf{Y}_i)}-\log\frac{\hat Y_{ij}}{g(\hat{\mathbf{Y}}_i)}\right)^2}\] Accounts for simplex geometry.
2. RMSE
\[\text{RMSE}_k = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(Y_{ij}-\hat Y_{ij})^2}\] Standard metric. Lower = better.

Conclusion

🌲
For compositional data, we adapt Random Forest splitting rules to respect the geometry of the simplex — avoiding the limitations and potential bias of traditional transformations to real space.
📊
Based on comparison results, the model shows strong and stable performance across a wide range of scenarios.
📦
Open-source implementation available as the DirichletRF R package on GitHub.
