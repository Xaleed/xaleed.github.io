An introduction to compositional data analysis

Why standard statistical methods fail on proportions, and how log-ratio transformations and the Dirichlet distribution offer a rigorous solution.

Compositional data appears whenever measurements represent parts of a whole — the proportion of species in an ecosystem, the allocation of a portfolio across assets, or the relative abundance of minerals in a rock sample. At first glance these look like ordinary multivariate data. They are not.

The problem with proportions

The fundamental constraint is that components sum to a constant, usually 1. This creates a simplex geometry that ordinary Euclidean methods ignore. Running a linear regression on raw proportions, for example, can produce predicted values outside $[0,1]$, and correlations between components are artificially constrained by the sum constraint — a phenomenon known as spurious correlation, identified by Pearson as early as 1897.

"The constant-sum constraint means that if one component increases, at least one other must decrease — regardless of any underlying relationship."

Log-ratio transformations

The solution, developed by John Aitchison in the 1980s, is to work in log-ratio space. The three most common transformations are:

  • ALR — additive log-ratio: $\text{alr}(\mathbf{x}) = \log(x_i / x_D)$ for a chosen reference component $x_D$
  • CLR — centred log-ratio: $\text{clr}(\mathbf{x}) = \log(x_i / g(\mathbf{x}))$ where $g(\mathbf{x})$ is the geometric mean
  • ILR — isometric log-ratio: an orthonormal basis representation that preserves distances

Once transformed, standard multivariate methods apply without modification. The ILR transformation is generally preferred for modelling because it produces an isometric embedding of the simplex in Euclidean space.

Example: a simple CLR in R

library(compositions)

# Simulate compositional data (3 parts, 100 observations)
x <- rDirichlet(100, alpha = c(2, 5, 3))

# Apply CLR transformation
x_clr <- clr(x)

# Now safe to use standard PCA
pca <- prcomp(x_clr, scale. = FALSE)
biplot(pca)

The Dirichlet distribution

For modelling compositional responses, the Dirichlet distribution is the natural choice. With concentration parameters $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_K)$, the density is:

$$f(\mathbf{x}; \boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha_0)}{\prod_{k=1}^K \Gamma(\alpha_k)} \prod_{k=1}^K x_k^{\alpha_k - 1}$$

where $\alpha_0 = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k$. The mean of component $k$ is $\alpha_k / \alpha_0$, and the variance structure is determined by the total concentration $\alpha_0$ — larger values produce distributions more concentrated around the mean.
