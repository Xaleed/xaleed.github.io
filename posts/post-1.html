An introduction to compositional data analysis

Why standard statistical methods fail on proportions, and how log-ratio transformations and the Dirichlet distribution offer a rigorous solution.

Compositional data appears whenever measurements represent parts of a whole — the proportion of species in an ecosystem, the allocation of a portfolio across assets, or the relative abundance of minerals in a rock sample. At first glance these look like ordinary multivariate data. They are not.

The problem with proportions

The fundamental constraint is that components sum to a constant, usually 1. This creates a simplex geometry that ordinary Euclidean methods ignore. Running a linear regression on raw proportions can produce predicted values outside $[0,1]$, and correlations between components are artificially constrained — a phenomenon known as spurious correlation.

"The constant-sum constraint means that if one component increases, at least one other must decrease — regardless of any underlying relationship."

Log-ratio transformations

The solution, developed by John Aitchison in the 1980s, is to work in log-ratio space. Once transformed, standard multivariate methods apply. The ILR transformation is generally preferred because it produces an isometric embedding of the simplex in Euclidean space:

$$\text{ilr}(\mathbf{x})_j = \sqrt{\frac{j}{j+1}} \log \frac{\left(\prod_{i=1}^j x_i\right)^{1/j}}{x_{j+1}}$$

Example in R

library(compositions)

x     <- rDirichlet(100, alpha = c(2, 5, 3))
x_clr <- clr(x)
pca   <- prcomp(x_clr, scale. = FALSE)
biplot(pca)
